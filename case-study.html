◆  The Problem

what we were solving

Enterprise compliance teams were drowning in manual, error-prone workflows — spending weeks doing work that should take hours.

Compliance teams buried in PDFs
Manual extraction from regulatory documents was taking 2–4 weeks per compliance cycle, with high error rates and duplicated effort across teams.
No visibility into gaps
Analysts had no centralized view of which requirements were met, partially covered, or completely missing — leading to costly last-minute scrambles during audits.
Zero design consistency
Each module was built by different teams at different times — the product felt like five separate tools stitched together.
◆  Research

understanding the people behind the process

I conducted contextual interviews with compliance analysts, risk officers, and platform administrators across our three Fortune 100 clients. Sessions lasted 60–90 minutes and included think-aloud walkthroughs of their current workflows.

Key insight: users weren't looking for automation to replace them — they wanted tools that made their expertise more powerful and reduced time spent on "data plumbing."

I synthesized findings into a journey map and three user archetypes that guided every subsequent design decision.

"I spend 70% of my day just pulling data from documents. By the time I get to actual analysis, I'm exhausted."
— Compliance Analyst, Citi
"I need to know at a glance: which requirements are red? I don't want to dig through spreadsheets to find out we missed something critical."
— Risk Manager, AbbVie

◆  Goals

what we set out to do

01
Reduce time-to-insight for compliance analysts
Surface the right information at the right moment — from AI-extracted data to gap heat maps — so analysts spend time on judgment, not retrieval.
02
Build a coherent, scalable design system
Create WorldKit — a token-based design system in Figma — so every module shares a visual language and future development is accelerated.
03
Make AI actions transparent and trustworthy
Design explainability into the AI layer — users must understand what the extraction engine found, why, and where the confidence is low.
04
Meet WCAG 2.2 AA accessibility standards
Built accessibility into the component library from day one rather than retrofitting.
◆  Feature · Extraction Engine

AI extraction that shows its work.

The extraction engine processes uploaded compliance documents using NLP models trained on regulatory text. Rather than just outputting extracted fields, I designed the interface to show the source span — users can hover any extracted value to see exactly which sentence it came from.

This was a direct response to a trust barrier uncovered in research: analysts wouldn't act on AI output unless they could verify it. Color-coded confidence bands (high / medium / low) let reviewers triage at a glance.

◆  Feature · Gap Analysis Dashboard

A heat map that makes risk impossible to miss.

The gap analysis module gives compliance officers a live view of which regulatory requirements are fully covered, partially addressed, or entirely missing — across all frameworks simultaneously.

Instead of a table, I designed a spatial heat map with drill-down capability. The heat map reduced time to identify critical gaps by 47% in moderated usability sessions.

Gap Analysis Dashboard
◆  Feature · Architecture Scanner

Scanning infrastructure, not just documents.

Beyond document-based compliance, clients needed to verify their deployed cloud architecture aligned with regulatory requirements. The architecture scanner connects to cloud providers and generates a live map of deployed resources overlaid with compliance status.

I introduced progressive disclosure — summary cards first, with an optional deep-dive view for technical users — and worked with engineers to define the most useful default abstraction level.

This project reinforced that explainability is a UX problem, not just an AI engineering problem. The biggest trust barrier wasn't the AI's accuracy — it was users not understanding how it reached its conclusions. Designing transparency into every layer was the single highest-impact design decision.


Building a design system in parallel with a live product requires enormous discipline — resisting the temptation to add one-off components and instead investing in the right abstractions that pay dividends across every feature built after.
