◆ CONTEXT

why operations mattered

7-Eleven operates 9,000+ stores nationwide, where store managers juggle inventory, staffing, and compliance from fragmented legacy tools.

While operations touch every part of the business, they ultimately flow through a single daily dashboard. This made the dashboard one of the highest leverage surfaces in the platform, where small improvements compound across thousands of stores.

The business wanted to reduce time-to-resolution on store issues, while managers consistently reported alert fatigue and unclear prioritization.

◆ GOALS

what we set out to do

Three goals shaped the redesign. Not all were equally visible to managers — all mattered to the business.

"Why do I find out about problems last?"

"How do we cut resolution time across 9,000 stores?"

"Can one dashboard replace four different logins?"

🎯
Reduce alert fatigue through smart prioritization

Give critical alerts visual dominance so managers immediately know what needs attention, and what can wait.

🎯
Cut average time-to-resolution by surfacing context immediately

Managers shouldn't need to switch apps to understand an alert. Everything needed to resolve an issue should appear in-context.

🎯
Unify inventory, staffing, and compliance into one dashboard

Four logins, four mental models, four places to miss something — collapse this into one coherent daily view.

◆ APPROACH

key product decisions

Before designing screens, we worked through the core tradeoffs that would shape every interaction in the platform.

📌
01
Severity should drive visual hierarchy, not chronology

The old list showed newest alerts first — making a 3-minute-old freezer failure invisible under a fresh parking lot report. We inverted this: severity is the primary sort, recency is secondary. The tradeoff: managers who expect "latest first" needed re-education, but resolution speed improved immediately.

📌
02
The dashboard should reflect what managers do, not what the system tracks

The system tracked 40+ data points per store. Managers made decisions on 6. We ruthlessly filtered the dashboard to the manager's mental model — not the engineering model. The tradeoff: some data became harder to access, but the primary workflow became dramatically faster.

📌
03
Alerts should resolve in-context, not require app-switching

The old workflow: see alert → open separate app → find the item → take action → log the action. We collapsed this to: see alert → tap → see context → resolve → auto-logged. The tradeoff: more complexity in the alert detail view, but the multi-app switching was eliminated entirely.

📌
04
Accountability should be automatic, not manually logged

Every resolved action creates an automatic audit log entry — who resolved it, when, and what action was taken. Managers hated logging; they'd skip it under pressure. Making it automatic meant audit trails went from 40% completion to 100% without adding any friction to the resolution flow.

◆ PRIORITIZATION

the most urgent thing, first

Alert fatigue isn't a volume problem — it's a hierarchy problem. When everything looks the same, nothing gets treated as urgent. We redesigned the alert list so severity is always the primary sort, with color, numbering, and visual weight reinforcing the order.

The decision. We chose to hide low-priority alerts behind a "Show all" toggle by default. Managers resisted at first — they worried about missing things. After two weeks, every manager in testing preferred it. Nothing important was missed; routine items were handled faster because critical items got handled first.
◆ UNIFIED CONTEXT

every system, one screen

The freezer alert and the inventory shortage are the same problem — a failed cooler means dairy spoilage which means a stock gap. The old system showed these as three separate notifications in three separate apps. We surfaced the connected context in a single view.

The decision. We pulled live inventory data into alert cards so managers could see the downstream impact of an issue immediately. The technical lift was significant — it required a real-time data bridge between three legacy systems — but resolution time dropped by 32% because managers stopped switching apps to gather context.

◆ REFLECTIONS

reflections & learnings

  • The most impactful decisions came from rethinking workflows, not redesigning screens.
  • Alert fatigue is a hierarchy problem, not a volume problem.
  • Consolidating systems mattered more to trust than visual polish.
  • The best operational tools disappear into the background until they're needed.
A note on this project

The hardest part wasn't designing the interface — it was convincing stakeholders that removing features (four apps down to one) was the right move. Every team wanted their data front and center. The real design challenge was negotiating information hierarchy across organizations, not pixels on a screen.
